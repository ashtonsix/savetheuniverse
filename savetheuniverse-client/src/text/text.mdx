<div id="introduction">

Across centuries of testing, from scales galactic to quantum, the second law of thermodynamics has stood seemingly invincible. It states heat flows only from where it is hot to where it is cold, fortelling a universe eventually bereft of tempreature gradients. Without which all processes, including life, shall halt.

In this research we introduce pseudo-momentum. A novel quantity that allows work to be performed even in the absence of pre-existing tempreature gradients, violating the second law of thermodynamics. We hypothesize the conservation of this quantity supersedes the conservation of momentum at sub-quantum scales beyond our current observational capacity.

We support this claim through a simulation, within which we create a quantum oscillator behaviourally identical to an electron. To further validate our simulation, we reproduce larger entities including whole atoms, and culminating in the simulation of simple molecules like water and methane. By reproducing seminal experiments, such as the double-slit experiment, we affirm our simulation's compatibility with special relativity and quantum mechanics. Running these simulations required sophisticated computational techniques, which we will detail.

The inspiration for this research stemmed from the study of artificial neural networks and general systems theory. We aim to paint an optimistic view of the universe's ultimate destiny. And we envision applications for the work across fields such as nuclear energy, quantum computing, and drug discovery.

<h2 id="pseudo-momentum">Defining pseudo-momentum</h2>

We employ a toy to introduce pseudo-momentum, a novel quantity derived from the introduction of pseudo-elastic collisions. While the toy does not model any known physical laws, it provides a clear demonstration of emergent behaviour related to pseudo-momentum.

In our toy, particles follow straight-line paths at constant velocity. Collisions with the boundary are perfectly elastic, while particle-particle collisions are pseudo-elastic. All particles share equal mass.

The equation governing particle-particle collisions is given by:

$$
\displaylines{
	\mathbf{J} = \frac{(1 + E)(\mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2)}{2} \frac{\langle \mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2\rangle}{\langle \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2, \mathbf{x}_1 - \mathbf{x}_2\rangle}, \\
	{\color{blue}S = \frac{\|\mathbf{v}_1\| + \|\mathbf{v}_2\|}{\|\mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{J}\| + \|\mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{J}\|}}, \\
	\mathbf{v}'_1 = {\color{blue}S} (\mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{J}), \\
	\mathbf{v}'_2 = {\color{blue}S} (\mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{J}),
}
$$

where $\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ denote position and velocity respectively, $\mathbf{J}$ is the impulse, $E$ is the coefficient of pseudo-elasticity, and ${\color{blue}S}$ is a scaling factor introduced to preserve pseudo-momentum. In the absence of ${\color{blue}S}$, the equation reduces to the classical particle-particle collision equation.

For $E \lt 1$, particles' velocities align, exhibiting emergent order within the closed system of our toy. That's unlike physical processes that only produce a superficial reduction of disorder (such as the formation of our solar system). The difference in behaviour is primarily because kinetic energy is not converted into internal energy within the toy.

With or without ${\color{blue}S}$, the direction of total (pseudo-)momentum remains invariant. For $E = 1$, additional conservation symmetries are observed:

$$
\displaylines {
  \|\mathbf{p}_1\| + \|\mathbf{p}_2\| = \|\mathbf{p}'_1\| + \|\mathbf{p}'_2\|, \\
  \mathbf{p}_1 + \mathbf{p}_2 = \mathbf{p}'_1 + \mathbf{p}'_2,
}
$$

where $\mathbf{p}$ is (pseudo-)momentum. Classical physics loses symmetry $\|\mathbf{p}_1\| + \|\mathbf{p}_2\| = \|\mathbf{p}'_1\| + \|\mathbf{p}'_2\|$ for $E \neq 1$. However, the inclusion of ${\color{blue}S}$ retains it, leading us to define pseudo-momentum as this conserved quantity. In adopting pseudo-momentum, we forfeit the conservation of momentum $\mathbf{p}_1 + \mathbf{p}_2 = \mathbf{p}'_1 + \mathbf{p}'_2$ for $E \neq 1$.

From now onwards, we will use $\boldsymbol{\rho}$ to denote pseudo-momentum.

The dispersion function $D(\theta)$, where $\theta$ is the cosine similarity between $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$, is a strictly increasing function with constraint $D(\theta) > 0$. We characterise $D(\theta)$ with dispersion factor $\delta$ where, informally speaking, $\delta$ measures how much the pseudo-momentum will spread out each time step and is defined by:

$$
\delta \in \left\{-\theta:D(\theta)=\frac{\int_{-1}^{1}D(\theta) \, d\theta}{2}\right\}
$$

Functions that satisfy $D(\theta)$ include $-a\theta^2+0.5\theta+a+0.5$ and $1 / (1+e^{-6\theta-a})$, where the value of $\delta$ increases with $a$.
